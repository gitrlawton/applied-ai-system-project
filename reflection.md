# Profile Comparisons

## High-Energy Pop vs. Chill Lofi

These two profiles are basically opposites. The pop profile wanted high energy, happy mood, and a fast tempo, and it got exactly that — Sunrise City came in at #1 with a score of 12.85 out of 13, picking up the full genre and mood bonus on top of strong numbers across the board. The lofi profile wanted low energy, a slow tempo, and a chill mood, and Focus Flow landed at #1 with a 12.86. What's interesting is that both profiles got similarly high scores for their top result, just at completely different ends of the catalog. The system basically split the 20 songs into two camps and each profile got its own camp. There was almost zero overlap between the two top-five lists, which shows that energy is doing a lot of the sorting work even before genre gets involved.

## Energy-Sad Clash vs. Impossible Combo

Both of these profiles were designed to have at least one preference that couldn't be fully satisfied. The Energy-Sad Clash asked for high energy but a sad mood — and the sad song (Broken Hallelujah) still won at 10.43 even though its energy was way off. The genre and mood match together were worth 5 points, and nothing else in the catalog could make up that difference through energy alone. The Impossible Combo asked for classical and angry, which no song in the catalog is — so both categorical bonuses were zero for every song. Without those 5 points available to anyone, the whole ranking collapsed down to pure number-matching, and Iron Curtain won just because it had the angry mood and the closest energy. The big takeaway comparing these two is that even one categorical match (just mood, in Iron Curtain's case) was enough to completely change who wins.

## Lofi Numbers Metal Label vs. Lone Wolf Genre

These two show the genre weight behaving in completely opposite ways. In the Lofi Numbers Metal Label test, the genre label said metal but all the numeric targets pointed to lofi — and the metal song never appeared in the top five at all. The 3-point genre bonus wasn't enough to overcome how wrong Iron Curtain's actual sound was compared to what the numbers were asking for. In the Lone Wolf Genre test, there was only one blues song in the whole catalog, and it scored a perfect 13 out of 13 because the profile was tuned to match it exactly. So genre can be completely overruled by numbers, but it can also be the deciding factor when everything else lines up. The difference is whether the song behind the genre label actually sounds like what the user wants.

## Flat Midpoint vs. Deep Intense Rock

The Flat Midpoint profile set everything to average — 0.5 energy, 0.5 valence, middle-of-the-road everything — with jazz as the genre. Coffee Shop Stories dominated at 11.42 because it was the only jazz/relaxed song, so it got the full 5-point categorical head start and no other song could catch it on neutral numbers alone. The Deep Intense Rock profile was the opposite of flat — it pushed every preference to an extreme, asking for very high energy, very low acousticness, and a fast tempo. That kind of extreme profile actually produces more interesting and spread-out results because it's pulling hard in one direction and the songs either keep up or fall far behind. The flat profile basically handed the win to whoever had the right genre label, while the extreme profile made the numbers do real work.
